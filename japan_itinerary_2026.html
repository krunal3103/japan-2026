📷 Shibuya Crossing + Hachiko Statue
📌 How to do it
•
Walk out to the crossing from the hotel. The Hachiko exit of Shibuya Station is right there. Start at street level to feel the energy, then go up.
📷 Photo Guide
📷
Hachiko Statue — Side angle with Shibuya Station behind. Don't shoot straight on — the side profile with the station entrance creates depth. Late afternoon light is golden here.
📷
Shibuya Scramble from Starbucks Window (2F) — The Starbucks right at the crossing has seats facing the intersection. Arrive early to grab a window seat. Best shot: telephoto-style compression with the crowd below. Order a drink, take your time — aim for a few minutes BEFORE the light changes, then fire when it goes red and everyone floods in.
📷
Street Level Crossing — Stand still right in the middle of the crossing as people flow past you. Classic shot: you stationary, crowd blurred around you. Step to the middle, stay calm, shoot burst mode.
💡
For long exposure crowd movement, use a tripod or rest your camera on the railing. The best exposure is around 1–2 seconds at the peak of the crossing flow.

Shibuya Sky Booked ✓
📌 What to expect
•
Open-air rooftop on the Scramble Square building. Go through security, take the elevator to the 46F then escalators to the roof. Allow 45–60 mins up top. The roof has transparent floor panels — stand on them.
📷 Photo Guide
📷
The Corner Glass Platform — This is Shibuya Sky's most iconic spot. Get to the corner where the glass floor meets the open railing. Shoot downward through the glass with the street grid below. Best in the blue hour (right after sunset).
📷
Sunset Skyline — Face west. Tokyo stretches to the horizon. Shoot with the setting sun creating a warm gradient behind the buildings.
📷
Night City Lights — Stay until after dark (around 7pm). The Scramble Crossing below becomes a constellation of headlights and neon. Shoot directly down.
💡
Wear a windproof jacket — it's very exposed. Bring your phone for wide shots and zoom lens for compression shots of the distant city.

Incense Smoke + Worshippers — The bronze incense cauldron in the courtyard. People wave the smoke over themselves. Candid shots here are powerful — smoke swirling around hands and faces in morning light.

🌸 Chidorigafuchi Moat Rowboating Booked ✓
•

Cherry Blossom Tunnel — The moat path on the east side has a tunnel of sakura that arches over the walkway AND the water. This is where the petal-fall shots happen. Wait for wind — the petals float down and carpet the water surface.
📷
Sakura Petal Water Surface — Look straight down from the boat at the moat surface covered in floating petals. Shoot straight down for an abstract sakura-carpet pattern.
💡
It's your birthday — this is the most romantic sakura experience in Tokyo. Go early; the queues for boats get very long by mid-morning. Book in advance if possible.

🌸 Walk North Shore of Lake Kawaguchi
•
From the ryokan / Kawaguchiko Station, walk or take the bus to the north shore viewing areas. This side of the lake gives the clearest, most directly-facing views of Fuji. The wind is usually calm in the morning, creating perfect reflections.
📷
Fuji + Boats (Kawaguchiko Lakefront) — Small wooden fishing boats moored near the shore with Fuji in the background. Classic composition — shoot from low on the bank so the boats fill the foreground. Early-afternoon wind can disrupt reflections; morning is best.
📷
Fuji Reflection Symmetry — Find a completely still section of the lake. Shoot Fuji and its reflection as a perfect vertical symmetry — crop so the mountain and its mirror image are equal. Best when the water is glassy calm (early morning or late afternoon).
📷
Couple Walking with Fuji Background — Position your partner frame-left walking toward Fuji on the right. Fuji centered in the background, person slightly left of center creating diagonal tension in the frame.
2:00 PM
Mt. Fuji Panoramic Ropeway (Kachi Kachi Ropeway)
•
The ropeway runs from near Kawaguchiko Station up Mt. Tenjo. At the top there's a viewing platform with 360° views. There's also a famous tanuki (raccoon dog) story associated with the mountain — ask the staff.
📷
Torii Gate Framing Fuji — At the top of the ropeway there's a small Asama Shrine with a torii gate. Position yourself so the gate frames Fuji in the center background. Use a longer focal length to compress the scene.
📷
360° Lake Panorama — Shoot the full panorama from the observation deck. Lake Kawaguchi in the foreground, Fuji to the south. A stitched panorama here is spectacular.
📷
Portrait with Fuji Behind — Stand on the viewing deck with Fuji centered behind you. Use telephoto compression to make Fuji appear large behind the portrait. The key is distance — step toward the railing, shoot from farther back with a zoom lens.

🦌 Nara Park — Deer Walk
•
Over 1,200 sika deer roam freely through Nara Park. They are considered sacred and approach people readily, especially if you have deer crackers (shika-senbei, sold by vendors throughout the park for about ¥200). Buy a pack and prepare to be surrounded.
📷
Deer Bowing for Crackers — The iconic Nara shot. Hold crackers at waist height and wait — the deer have learned to bow their heads up toward the crackers. When they bow and then look up at you, their eyes are expressive and their antlers frame perfectly against the sakura/temple behind. Shoot in burst mode when they bow.
📷
Deer Under Cherry Blossoms — Walk toward the Kasuga Taisha area of the park where deer congregate under the sakura trees. Find a composition with deer grazing in the foreground and cherry trees forming a canopy above.
📷
Wide Shot with Todai-ji Behind — From the main park area, position yourself with deer in the immediate foreground and the massive wooden entrance gate of Todai-ji rising in the background.
💡
The deer are wild but gentle. Hold crackers slightly above waist height to get the bowing behavior. Don't tease them or they'll headbutt you — be calm and generous. A doe with young fawns in spring is one of the most beautiful wildlife photos you can get in Japan.

Todai-ji Temple — Great Buddha Hall
•
The Great Buddha Hall (Daibutsuden) is the largest wooden structure in the world. Inside sits an 18-meter bronze Buddha — one of the most impressive sights in Japan. Even if you've seen photos, the scale is shocking in person.
📷
Massive Wooden Entrance — Nandaimon Gate — The huge gate you pass through first has two enormous guardian figures (Nio) in each alcove. Shoot looking up through the gate — the scale is extraordinary. The guardians' expressions are detailed and intense.
📷
Bronze Buddha Interior Wide Shot — Once inside, shoot from the back corner of the hall to get the full Buddha statue with the hall's wooden pillars framing it. The Buddha's face is detailed and serene — a close-up portrait shot works beautifully too.

📷 Dotonbori Canal — Sunset & Blue Hour
•

🏮 CRITICAL — Fushimi Inari Taisha Sunrise
•
Walk from Toshiharu Ryokan to the first torii gate (3–5 min). Walk briskly through the first long torii tunnel without stopping — your goal is the deeper sections where crowds are nonexistent. The shrine is open 24 hours. At 6am you will share the mountain path with only a handful of other early risers.
•
Strategy: Walk 10–15 minutes in before starting to photograph. The tourist crowds never reach more than 200m into the gate path — beyond that, you have the mountain largely to yourself. The deeper paths toward Yotsutsuji viewpoint (about 30 min up) are the best for empty shots.
💡
Best shot location: Walk 15–20 minutes uphill past the main Fushimi Inari shrine building. This is where the torii tunnel becomes completely empty and the gates narrow into a tight symmetrical corridor — no souvenir stalls, no crowds, just an endless tunnel of vermillion receding into the mountain forest. This specific section is what every iconic Fushimi Inari photo is taken from. Don't stop at the first section — keep walking.
📷
Endless Vermillion Gates — Deep Torii Tunnel — Walk deep into the gate path (past the first busy section). The gates narrow and the path curves — find a straight section and shoot looking straight down the tunnel. Perfect centered symmetry with the orange gates receding to a point.
📷
Torii Gate Tunnel Curve — Find a section where the gate path bends — the gates bending along the mountainside curve create a beautiful curving composition. Shoot from inside the bend.
📷
Soft Morning Forest Light Through Gates — The gaps between the gates let in shafts of morning light from the east. Find a spot where a shaft of golden light crosses the path between two gates — walk through it and shoot the light from the shadowed section of the path.
📷
Yotsutsuji Viewpoint (Optional Extra Climb ~30 min) — If you have the energy, continue to the Yotsutsuji crossroads viewpoint. From here you can see Kyoto spread below with torii gates in the foreground. A wide-angle shot with gates framing the sleeping city below is one of the best viewpoints in Japan.
💡
The torii path is always red — but at dawn, with soft directional light, the vermillion turns warm amber. After 9am direct sunlight flattens the color. The difference between a 6am photo and a 10am photo here is enormous.
