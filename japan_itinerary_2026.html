Arrival Day — Shibuya
Tokyo Neighborhood: Shibuya 🏨 Shibuya Excel Hotel Tokyo 👟 ~8,000–10,000 steps · 🟢 Easy✈️ Arrival from Seoul — no trains today
✈️
✈️ Arrival Day — eat on the flight or at airport
💡
You're arriving from Seoul at 3pm — lunch is on the flight or at Incheon/Gimpo airport before boarding. Don't worry about lunch on arrival day. Streamer Coffee after check-in is your first Tokyo food stop, then shopping snacks at Scramble Square B2F, then dinner.
3:00 PM
Hotel Check-in, Refresh & First Coffee
☕
Streamer Coffee Company (Shibuya) — Tokyo's most Instagram-famous latte art spot, 5-minute walk from the hotel. The baristas are latte art competition champions — watch them work. Order a flat white or seasonal latte. The perfect first Tokyo moment after checking in.
📌 What to do
•
Drop bags, freshen up — jet lag is real. Give yourself a full hour before heading out. The hotel is in the thick of Shibuya so you can walk everywhere tonight.
4:15 PM
📷 Shibuya Crossing + Hachiko Statue
📌 How to do it
•
Walk out to the crossing from the hotel. The Hachiko exit of Shibuya Station is right there. Start at street level to feel the energy, then go up.
📷 Photo Guide
📷
Hachiko Statue — Side angle with Shibuya Station behind. Don't shoot straight on — the side profile with the station entrance creates depth. Late afternoon light is golden here.
📷
Shibuya Scramble from Starbucks Window (2F) — The Starbucks right at the crossing has seats facing the intersection. Arrive early to grab a window seat. Best shot: telephoto-style compression with the crowd below. Order a drink, take your time — aim for a few minutes BEFORE the light changes, then fire when it goes red and everyone floods in.
📷
Street Level Crossing — Stand still right in the middle of the crossing as people flow past you. Classic shot: you stationary, crowd blurred around you. Step to the middle, stay calm, shoot burst mode.
💡
For long exposure crowd movement, use a tripod or rest your camera on the railing. The best exposure is around 1–2 seconds at the peak of the crossing flow.
5:00 PM
Shibuya Sky Booked ✓
📌 What to expect
•
Open-air rooftop on the Scramble Square building. Go through security, take the elevator to the 46F then escalators to the roof. Allow 45–60 mins up top. The roof has transparent floor panels — stand on them.
📷 Photo Guide
📷
The Corner Glass Platform — This is Shibuya Sky's most iconic spot. Get to the corner where the glass floor meets the open railing. Shoot downward through the glass with the street grid below. Best in the blue hour (right after sunset).
📷
Sunset Skyline — Face west. Tokyo stretches to the horizon. Shoot with the setting sun creating a warm gradient behind the buildings.
📷
Night City Lights — Stay until after dark (around 7pm). The Scramble Crossing below becomes a constellation of headlights and neon. Shoot directly down.
💡
Wear a windproof jacket — it's very exposed. Bring your phone for wide shots and zoom lens for compression shots of the distant city.

Shibuya Shopping + B2F Snack
🧀
Scramble Square B2F Food Floor — Grab a warm cheese tart from BAKE Cheese Tart (molten inside, crispy pastry outside) or a cream puff from Beard Papa — eat it on the spot. The Scramble Square food basement is directly above the crossing. A great little Japan-only snack moment.

📷 Meiji Shrine
•
Enter from the Harajuku entrance on the south side (5 min walk from Harajuku Station). The path through the forest to the shrine takes about 10 minutes. Stop and listen — the silence is remarkable for being in central Tokyo.
📷
Giant Torii Gate (First Gate) — Stand far back and shoot toward the gate with the forested path leading in. Best when there are a few people walking through for scale — they make the gate look enormous.
📷
Forest Path — The gravel path lined by 50-year-old trees. Shoot looking down the path in the morning light with dappled shadows. Use a low angle to make the trees loom larger.
📷
Sake Barrel Wall — Just inside the first gate on the left is a wall of colorful ceremonial sake barrels. Wide shot shows the full wall, or close up on the painted labels.
🔖
Goshuin Stamp ✓ — Meiji Shrine offers one of Tokyo's most beautiful goshuin stamps. The stamp desk (朱印所) is inside the main shrine complex, just past the main torii gate on the right. Open from 9am. Cost ¥500. The calligraphy is written fresh by hand — watch them do it. This is a great first stamp if you're starting your goshuincho here.

Lantern Alley — Shoot from inside the alley looking toward the lit-up entrance. Wide aperture for bokeh background of lanterns. Candid shots of the grill chefs at work — close up on hands and flames.
📷
Narrow Yakitori Streets — The cramped counters and chefs at work are great candid subjects. Sit at a counter and photograph the grill and the chefs' hands working — close up, wide aperture, warm tones.

7:30 AM
🍳 Kappabashi Kitchen Street — Morning Browse
•
Take the train to Tawaramachi Station (Ginza Line from Asakusa, 1 stop) — the entrance to Kappabashi-dori, Tokyo's professional kitchen supply street. A 400m street of wholesale kitchen and restaurant supply shops open to the public. Go early (most shops open by 9am) before the tourist crowds and the full heat of the day.
•
Must stops on Kappabashi: Kama-asa (est. 1908) for knives and the hinoki cypress chopping board — their knife engraving service takes about 30 minutes so drop it off first then browse while you wait. Tsubaya for hand-forged specialist knives. The plastic food sample shops for iconic Japanese souvenir keychains.
•
Also look for: Japanese iron tetsubin kettles, matcha whisks (chasen) and tea sets, beautiful lacquerware, ceramic plates, and chopstick rests. Everything here is professional quality at trade prices.
💡
Drop off the knife for engraving at Kama-asa first thing — they'll have it ready by the time you've finished browsing the rest of the street. Budget ¥10,000–50,000+ for the knife, ¥5,000–20,000 for the chopping board. Give yourself 60–75 minutes here.

🌸 Senso-ji Temple — Morning Visit
•
Arrive before 9am to beat the crowds. The main gate (Kaminarimon) and the approach to the temple are magical in the morning light with far fewer tourists. By 10am it gets very crowded.
📷
Kaminarimon Gate Lantern — The giant red lantern at the main gate. Best shot: frame it from dead center of the path looking straight through with the temple visible beyond. Morning light hits it from the east and creates beautiful warm tones.
📷
Five-Story Pagoda from Below — Walk to the right side of the temple courtyard. The pagoda rises to the left of the main hall. Shoot from below at a low angle looking up — compress the storeys against the sky.
📷
Incense Smoke + Worshippers — The bronze incense cauldron in the courtyard. People wave the smoke over themselves. Candid shots here are powerful — smoke swirling around hands and faces in morning light.
🔖
Goshuin Stamp ✓ — Senso-ji is one of the most famous goshuin stamps in Japan. The stamp office is inside the main hall on the left side. Opens at 8am. Cost ¥300. They also sell their own beautiful goshuincho books here (red with gold Kaminarimon design) — if you haven't bought one in Seoul, buy it here first thing. This is a great stamp to start your book with.
💡
Soft morning light is genuinely beautiful here — this is not a cliché. Show up early and you'll feel it.
9:30 AM
Nakamise Street — Snack Crawl
•
The shopping street leading to Senso-ji is lined with traditional snack and souvenir stalls. This is where you do a slow snack crawl. Budget 30–45 minutes.
🍵
Chaya Tabenochi — Matcha crème brûlée. A must. The brulee is thick and the matcha is intense. Usually a short queue — worth it.
🍦

🍡

🍠
Imo Pippi — Sweet potato Mont Blanc / brûlée. Rich and creamy. Grab this as your last snack before heading to the park.
10:15 AM
🥞 Benitsuru — Asakusa Pancakes (optional)
🌿
Benitsuru (紅鶴) — One of Asakusa's most beloved pancake spots. Famous for thick, fluffy Japanese hotcakes served with butter and maple syrup. A very different texture from Western pancakes — almost soufflé-like, slightly sweet, impossibly light. The Asakusa location is a short walk from Nakamise Street. Vegetarian friendly. Can have a short queue — if it's long, skip and continue to Sumida Park. Budget 30 minutes if you go.
💡
This is optional — you've already had melon pan for breakfast and done a Nakamise snack crawl. Only stop here if you're still hungry or specifically want the Benitsuru experience. It's worth it if you can fit it in without rushing Sumida Park.

🌸 Sumida Park Sakura Walk
•
Walk from Senso-ji north along the Sumida River. Sumida Park is just 5 minutes on foot. The riverside is lined with hundreds of sakura trees with Tokyo Skytree as a backdrop.
📷
Skytree + Sakura — THE SHOT — Find a spot on the west bank of the river looking northeast. The Skytree rises behind a foreground of full-bloom sakura trees. This is one of Tokyo's most photographed sakura compositions. Shoot in the morning when the tree shadows fall away from you and the sky is clear.
📷
Riverside Sakura Canopy — Walk under the sakura trees along the riverbank. Shoot looking along the river with a tunnel of pink overhead and the river below.
💡
Morning is best here — by midday the light is flat. If it's overcast, the diffused light on the pink blossoms is still beautiful. Shoot both directions along the river.

4:00 PM
✨ teamLab Planets TOKYO Booked ✓
•
You take your shoes off at the entrance. The experience is deeply immersive — you walk through water, lie in a mirror room, and disappear into digital flowers. Allow 60–90 minutes. Don't rush.
📷
Infinity Mirror Light Room — The most-photographed room. The floor is a mirror and the room is filled with hanging floating lights. Key technique: use portrait mode or a wide aperture to let the reflections blur into orbs. Stand still, shoot slow exposures (1–2 sec on a timer). The water underfoot creates perfect floor reflections.
📷
Water Reflection Installation — You walk through shallow water with projections on the floor below you. Silhouette shots work beautifully here — stand still with the projection swirling around your feet.
💡
Wear neutral / dark clothing — whites and brights compete with the light installations. Dark navy or black clothing lets you blend into the projections and makes photos look more ethereal. Wear clothes you don't mind getting slightly wet — the water floor is a few cm deep.

DiverCity Tokyo Plaza — Gundam Statue (Odaiba)
🤖

🎎 Katsuoji Temple — Daruma Doll Temple (Option A only)
•
Nestled in the forested mountains of Minoh, north Osaka. A 1,300-year-old temple famous for thousands of Daruma dolls scattered across every surface — tucked into rocks, lining staircases, hanging from branches, peeking out from moss. Unlike anything else in Japan. Temple entry ¥500. Opens at 8am — arrive right at opening for the quietest visit. Budget 90 minutes here.
•
The Daruma ritual: At the Granting Office, choose a Daruma that feels right to you. Write your goal or life purpose on the back. Paint in the RIGHT eye as a personal vow — "I will work toward this." Bring the one-eyed Daruma home. When you achieve your goal, fill in the LEFT eye yourself. A genuinely moving ritual, not just a tourist activity.
📷
Daruma Staircase — The temple's most iconic shot: stone steps completely lined with red Daruma dolls on both sides, leading up into the forested hillside. Shoot from below looking up — the repetition of hundreds of round red faces receding into the distance is extraordinary. Early morning with no crowds is the only time to get this clean.
📷
Mist Bridge over the Pond — The entrance bridge crosses a koi pond often shrouded in morning mist. The red bridge, misty forest, and occasional sakura in early April is a very painterly composition.
📷
Daruma Hidden in Moss and Rocks — Walk slowly and look for Daruma tucked into unlikely places — inside tree hollows, between rocks, perched on branches. Close-up macro shots of a single Daruma face surrounded by moss are beautiful.
📷
Spring at Katsuoji — In early April, cherry blossoms bloom throughout the 26,000m² grounds. Daruma dolls + sakura + forest mist = one of the most uniquely Japanese scenes you'll photograph on this whole trip.
🔖
Goshuin Stamp ✓ — Katsuoji's goshuin stamp has become famous — it features the Daruma design and is one of the most viral temple stamps in Japan. The stamp office opens at 8am. Cost ¥500. One of the most distinctive stamps you'll collect on this trip.
💡
The Daruma staircase photo can have queues later in the day — at 8am you'll have it entirely to yourself. Don't rush the grounds — there are Daruma hidden in spots most visitors walk straight past. Look in every crevice.

🏮 Hozenji Yokocho — Hidden Alley Temple
•

At the end sits Hozen-ji Temple — a tiny shrine with a famous moss-covered Fudo Myo-o statue that worshippers continuously pour water over, turning it completely green. A genuinely magical hidden spot that most tourists miss entirely.

🏮 CRITICAL — Fushimi Inari Taisha Sunrise
•
Walk from Toshiharu Ryokan to the first torii gate (3–5 min). Walk briskly through the first long torii tunnel without stopping — your goal is the deeper sections where crowds are nonexistent. The shrine is open 24 hours. At 6am you will share the mountain path with only a handful of other early risers.
•
Strategy: Walk 10–15 minutes in before starting to photograph. The tourist crowds never reach more than 200m into the gate path — beyond that, you have the mountain largely to yourself. The deeper paths toward Yotsutsuji viewpoint (about 30 min up) are the best for empty shots.
💡
Best shot location: Walk 15–20 minutes uphill past the main Fushimi Inari shrine building. This is where the torii tunnel becomes completely empty and the gates narrow into a tight symmetrical corridor — no souvenir stalls, no crowds, just an endless tunnel of vermillion receding into the mountain forest. This specific section is what every iconic Fushimi Inari photo is taken from. Don't stop at the first section — keep walking.
📷
Endless Vermillion Gates — Deep Torii Tunnel — Walk deep into the gate path (past the first busy section). The gates narrow and the path curves — find a straight section and shoot looking straight down the tunnel. Perfect centered symmetry with the orange gates receding to a point.
📷
Torii Gate Tunnel Curve — Find a section where the gate path bends — the gates bending along the mountainside curve create a beautiful curving composition. Shoot from inside the bend.
📷
Soft Morning Forest Light Through Gates — The gaps between the gates let in shafts of morning light from the east. Find a spot where a shaft of golden light crosses the path between two gates — walk through it and shoot the light from the shadowed section of the path.
📷
Yotsutsuji Viewpoint (Optional Extra Climb ~30 min) — If you have the energy, continue to the Yotsutsuji crossroads viewpoint. From here you can see Kyoto spread below with torii gates in the foreground. A wide-angle shot with gates framing the sleeping city below is one of the best viewpoints in Japan.
💡
The torii path is always red — but at dawn, with soft directional light, the vermillion turns warm amber. After 9am direct sunlight flattens the color. The difference between a 6am photo and a 10am photo here is enormous.
🔖
Goshuin Stamp ✓ — The most special stamp of the whole trip — Fushimi Inari Taisha offers a goshuin stamp at the main shrine office at the base of the torii path. Get it on your way back down after the sunrise photography — the office opens at 8am so timing is perfect. Cost ¥300. The stamp features the iconic vermillion torii gate design with the shrine's fox (kitsune) motif. You can also get a second special stamp at the Okusha sub-shrine further up the mountain if you climb to the Yotsutsuji viewpoint. The most fitting stamp to end your Kyoto collection with — the last morning, the most iconic shrine. 🦊

•

1:30 PM
🗿 Great Buddha of Kamakura (Kotoku-in)
•
Take the Enoden line from Kamakura Station to Hase Station (7 minutes, ¥200) — the Enoden is a charming narrow-gauge coastal train that squeezes through residential streets and runs right beside the ocean. From Hase Station it's a 7-minute walk to the Great Buddha. Entry ¥300 — the grounds are open air. The bronze Buddha is 13.35 metres tall and has been sitting here since 1252. You can go inside the hollow statue for ¥20 extra.
📷
Great Buddha Wide Shot — Stand far back to capture the full Buddha against the forested hillside behind. The contrast of the ancient bronze against the green mountains is the classic shot. Also shoot close from below looking up at the face — the detail of the casting is extraordinary up close.
📷
Enoden Train + Ocean — On the way to Hase, shoot from the Enoden window as the train runs alongside the ocean. The narrow retro train with the Pacific spread out beside it is one of Japan's most iconic train photography spots.
🔖
Goshuin Stamp ✓ — Kotoku-in (Great Buddha temple) offers a goshuin stamp at the stamp office near the entrance gate. Cost ¥300. One of the most unique stamps in Japan — features the Great Buddha design.
3:00 PM
🌊 Hasedera Temple + Kamakura Coastline
•
A 7-minute walk from the Great Buddha. Hasedera is built into the hillside above Kamakura — the upper garden terrace has sweeping views over the town and out to the Pacific Ocean. One of Kamakura's most beautiful temples. ¥400 entry.
📷
Ocean View from Temple Garden — From the upper terrace, shoot looking over the traditional temple rooflines toward the Pacific. On a clear April day, the combination of ocean, temple, and possible cherry blossoms is extraordinary.
•
After Hasedera, walk down to Yuigahama Beach — just 10 minutes on foot. The beach is beautiful and quiet in April (too early for summer crowds). Sit at a beachside café for coffee with an ocean view. Very different energy from anything else on this trip.
